\section{Problem \& Motivation}

\begin{frame}{The problem}
  \textbf{One hospital, one week, an elective waiting list bigger than the
  week's capacity.}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Decide, for each waiting case: which room, what time, which day,
      which surgeon -- or leave it for a later week.
    \item Has to be \textbf{feasible}: rooms, surgeons, and a handful of
      shared resources are all finite.
    \item Has to be \textbf{fair}: a genuinely urgent patient shouldn't sit
      behind a routine one just because the routine case reached the front
      of a FIFO queue first.
  \end{itemize}
  \vspace{1em}
  \pause
  \textbf{Brief's own framing:} room hours, surgeon availability, shared
  equipment, turnover between cases, downstream bed pressure -- explicitly
  invites simplification, not a full hospital simulation.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Why it matters -- this isn't a toy problem}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{OR time is expensive and perishable.} $\sim\$30$--$40$ per
      minute once staffing/equipment/overhead are amortized, one widely
      cited U.S. estimate (Macario, 2010) -- and an idle OR-minute today
      can't be banked for tomorrow.
    \item \textbf{This is a recognized sub-problem.} Cardoen, Demeulemeester
      \& Beli\"en (2010) frame ``advance scheduling'' -- deciding which case
      runs on which day -- as distinct from same-day sequencing or
      disruption handling. That's the scope of this model.
    \item \textbf{Elective and emergency demand compete for the same
      rooms} (Van Riet \& Demeulemeester, 2015) -- the reason an
      emergency-add-on priority tier exists in the model at all.
    \item \textbf{The waiting list itself is the audited number} -- not just
      a general claim. Next slide: the specific system this model's
      priority mechanism is grounded in.
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{The Portuguese case: SIGIC}
  \textbf{Sistema Integrado de Gest\~ao de Inscritos para Cirurgia} --
  Portugal's national system for managing the elective surgical waiting
  list, established by \emph{Portaria n.\textsuperscript{o} 45/2008}.
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Every elective case is assigned one of \textbf{four clinical
      priority tiers}, each with its own maximum acceptable wait:
      \textbf{270 / 60 / 15 / 3 days}.
    \item This is exactly the same tier structure used by the UK NHS's
      Referral-to-Treatment targets and several Canadian provincial
      wait-time benchmarks: tiered deadlines, audited breach rates -- not
      a single undifferentiated FIFO queue.
  \end{itemize}
  \pause
  \textbf{Why this matters here:} these four numbers are not illustrative
  defaults invented for this demo -- they are \emph{this model's} maximum-wait
  parameters, $\text{wl}^{max}_p$, taken directly from a real, audited policy.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{The Portuguese case: what the 2016 audit found}
  Marques \& Captivo (2015) studied the Centro Hospitalar Lisboa Norte
  (CHLN) waiting list against the SIGIC targets above:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item $\sim$\textbf{7{,}400} patients on the list at audit time.
    \item \textbf{16\%} had \emph{already} breached their tier's deadline.
    \item Among breached cases, the average overrun was \textbf{147 days}
      hospital-wide -- and \textbf{$\sim$261 days} specifically in
      Neurosurgery, the worst-performing service.
  \end{itemize}
  \pause
  \begin{block}{The point for this model}
    Breach penalties in the objective ($w_c$, Term 3) aren't a cosmetic
    addition -- they encode \emph{exactly} the number a real system using
    this policy is actually graded on. A model that only maximizes
    throughput could report a great utilization number while quietly
    reproducing this audit's failure mode.
  \end{block}
\end{frame}

\section{Scope \& Assumptions}

\begin{frame}{What's kept, what's left out}
  \textbf{Kept:} case$\to$room$\to$time assignment, surgeon availability \&
  workload limits, room/service rosters, one shared piece of equipment, a
  downstream recovery/ICU-bed constraint.
  \vspace{0.5em}
  \begin{columns}
    \begin{column}{0.5\textwidth}
      \textbf{Left out, deliberately:}
      \begin{itemize}
        \footnotesize
        \item Stochastic durations
        \item Same-day disruption
        \item Nurses/anaesthetists as separate resources
      \end{itemize}
    \end{column}
    \begin{column}{0.5\textwidth}
      \begin{itemize}
        \footnotesize
        \item Sequence-dependent cleaning
        \item Multi-week planning
        \item An explicit emergency capacity reserve
      \end{itemize}
    \end{column}
  \end{columns}
  \backbutton
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Key assumptions (1/2)}
  \small
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Deterministic durations.} One estimated duration per case.
      Real durations are stochastic and biased \emph{long} -- the standard
      tractable starting point (Denton et al., 2010); stochastic extension
      named, not attempted.
    \item \textbf{Turnover depends on the case's own duration}, bucketed
      (15/25/40 min), not on which two cases are adjacent. The
      sequence-dependent piece is real but secondary here (CP Optimizer
      appendix shows how it would work).
    \item \textbf{Surgeons are the binding staffing resource.} Nurses/
      anaesthetists assumed bundled with the room's roster -- true whenever
      the surgeon's calendar, not support staff's, is the actual
      bottleneck.
    \item \textbf{One occurrence per patient per week.} Conservative
      default; combining same-day multi-procedure cases is service-specific
      clinical judgment, not assumed here.
  \end{enumerate}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Key assumptions (2/2)}
  \small
  \begin{enumerate}
    \setcounter{enumi}{4}
    \item \textbf{Bed capacity is constant across the week}, with an
      \emph{explicit} overflow penalty for a stay crossing the horizon
      boundary -- not a silent approximation either way.
    \item \textbf{One ad hoc institutional rule (pediatric block)} included
      as a worked example: costs nothing structurally, just one more
      eligibility predicate.
    \item \textbf{Single week, single hospital, no same-day disruption, no
      explicit emergency reserve.} A genuinely different problem
      (reactive rescheduling) from advance scheduling.
  \end{enumerate}
  \vspace{1em}
  \footnotesize Full reasoning, trade-offs, and what relaxing each one would
  require: FORMULATION.md \S2.
\end{frame}

\section{The CP-SAT Model, in Depth}

\begin{frame}{Sets and indices}
  \begin{center}
  \begin{tabular}{cl}
    \toprule
    Symbol & Meaning \\
    \midrule
    $c \in C$ & Surgical cases (one per patient-procedure pair) \\
    $d \in D$ & Planning days, $D=\{1,\dots,5\}$ \\
    $r \in R$ & Operating rooms \\
    $h \in H$ & Surgeons \\
    $e \in E$ & Shared equipment types \\
    $D_c \subseteq D$ & Days $c$ may run on ($\{1\}$ if must-run-today) \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
  \end{center}
  \backbutton
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Decision variables -- presence and disposition}
  For every $(c,d,r)$ surviving eligibility (room-service roster,
  ambulatory-only, pediatric block, surgeon availability):
  \[
    \text{pr}_{cdr}\in\{0,1\} \qquad \text{start}_{cdr},\,\text{end}_{cdr}\in[0,k_{dr}]
  \]
  For every case that isn't priority-4, an unscheduled flag:
  \[
    u_c\in\{0,1\}, \qquad \sum_{d,r}\text{pr}_{cdr} + u_c = 1
  \]
  \footnotesize Candidates are only generated for $(c,d,r)$ triples that
  pass eligibility -- the model's main variable-count reduction, done once
  up front, never re-checked by a constraint later.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Decision variables -- why \emph{two} intervals}
  \[
    \text{iv}_{cdr} = \texttt{NewOptionalIntervalVar}(\text{start}_{cdr},\,t_c^{tot},\,\text{end}_{cdr},\,\text{pr}_{cdr})
    \quad\text{ROOM, size } t_c^{op}+t_c^{clean}
  \]
  \[
    \text{sgiv}_{cdr} = \texttt{NewOptionalIntervalVar}(\text{start}_{cdr},\,t_c^{op},\,\text{sgend}_{cdr},\,\text{pr}_{cdr})
    \quad\text{SURGEON, size } t_c^{op}
  \]
  Both share \texttt{start}; both are \emph{present} exactly when
  $\text{pr}_{cdr}=1$.
  \pause
  \begin{block}{Worked example}
    Surgeon H operates Case X in Room 1: $t_c^{op}=70$, $t_c^{clean}=20$,
    starting at $t=0$. The room stays blocked until $t=90$. The surgeon's
    own involvement ends at $t=70$ -- they can scrub into Room 2 for Case Y
    while nursing staff finish cleaning Room 1. One interval sized
    $t_c^{tot}$ for C7 and a \emph{separate} one sized $t_c^{op}$ for C8 is
    what lets the model see that; collapsing both onto the room's longer
    interval would wrongly forbid it.
  \end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Objective function}
  \[
    \min \;
    \underbrace{\sum_{c:dd_c\ge0}\sum_{d\in D_c,r}[dd_c+d]\,\text{pr}_{cdr}}_{\text{on-time, prefer earlier}}
    +
    \underbrace{\sum_{c:dd_c<0}\sum_{d\in D_c,r}[dd_c+\alpha d]\,\text{pr}_{cdr}}_{\text{overdue, urgency-weighted}}
  \]
  \[
    {}+\;
    \underbrace{\sum_{c:p_c\ne4} w_c\,u_c}_{\text{non-scheduling penalty}}
    +
    \underbrace{\pi^{ovf}\sum_{c:\rho(c)\ne\text{none}}\text{overflow}_c}_{\text{bed-overflow penalty}}
  \]
  \[
    w_c = \mu_{p_c}\cdot\text{PenaltyCurve}(dd_c) + 1.2\cdot\max_{c'} dd_{c'}
  \]
  \footnotesize \texttt{PenaltyCurve} is flat while a case has slack, then
  escalates sharply past the deadline -- the same shape the SIGIC-style
  systems above use to make breaches expensive, not merely ``less
  preferred''. Term 3 is built to dominate Terms 1--2 (proof: \S5 of this
  deck) -- the model only drops a case when there's genuinely no room.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Constraints C1--C3 -- case disposition}
  \textbf{C1 -- one occurrence per patient per week:}
  \[
    \sum_{c:\text{patient}(c)=n}\sum_{d,r}\text{pr}_{cdr} \le 1 \qquad \forall n
  \]
  \textbf{C2 -- priority-4 locked to day 1:}
  \[
    \sum_r \text{pr}_{c,1,r} = 1 \quad\text{(every other slot for $c$ forced to 0)}
  \]
  \textbf{C3 -- schedule-or-penalize:} already written into the $u_c$
  definition -- $\sum_{d,r}\text{pr}_{cdr}+u_c=1$.
  \vspace{1em}

  \footnotesize C4--C6 (room-service roster, ambulatory-only, the
  pediatric block) are resolved during candidate generation, before any of
  the above -- they never appear as a constraint row at all.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Constraints C7--C9 -- room and surgeon}
  \textbf{C7 -- room, exact non-overlap:}
  \[
    \texttt{AddNoOverlap}\big(\{\text{iv}_{cdr}: d,r\text{ fixed}\}\big) \qquad \forall d,r
  \]
  \textbf{C8 -- surgeon, exact non-overlap on their own window, plus a
  daily cap:}
  \[
    \texttt{AddNoOverlap}\big(\{\text{sgiv}_{cdr}\}\big), \qquad
    \sum_{c:\text{surgeon}(c)=h}\sum_r t_c^{op}\,\text{pr}_{cdr}\le k_{hd}
  \]
  \textbf{C9 -- surgeon weekly limit:}
  \[
    \sum_{c:\text{surgeon}(c)=h}\sum_{d,r} t_c^{op}\,\text{pr}_{cdr}\le k_h \qquad \forall h
  \]
  \footnotesize NoOverlap alone bounds concurrency, not total hours -- C8/C9's
  minute caps are kept alongside it for exactly that reason.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Constraints C10--C11 -- equipment and beds}
  \textbf{C10 -- shared equipment, exact concurrency:}
  \[
    \texttt{AddCumulative}\big(\{\text{iv}_{cdr}: u_{ce}=1,\,d\text{ fixed}\},\,1,\,\kappa_{ed}\big) \qquad \forall e,d
  \]
  \textbf{C11 -- recovery/ICU beds, day-granularity, with overflow:}
  \[
    \text{bed}_c = \texttt{NewOptionalIntervalVar}(\text{dayof}_c,\,\text{los}_c,\,\text{dayof}_c+\text{los}_c,\,1-u_c)
  \]
  \[
    \texttt{AddCumulative}\big(\{\text{bed}_c:\rho(c)=\rho\},\,1,\,\beta_\rho\big) \qquad \forall\rho
  \]
  \[
    \text{overflow}_c = \max\big(0,\ (\text{dayof}_c+\text{los}_c)-n_{days}\big)
  \]
  \footnotesize A multi-day stay needs a real ``day of surgery'' value to
  start counting from -- the concrete reason this model is interval-based
  at all, not just a stylistic choice.
  \backbutton
\end{frame}

\section{Parameter Calibration}

\begin{frame}{How every default was chosen -- four honest categories}
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Literature-grounded} -- taken from a real, published
      policy or study (e.g.\ SIGIC's max-wait days).
    \item \textbf{Derivable rule} -- computed from other parameters by an
      explicit, checkable formula (e.g.\ the priority multipliers).
    \item \textbf{Demo-design choice} -- sized specifically to make the
      demo data exercise a constraint, not measured from any one hospital.
    \item \textbf{Placeholder} -- stands in for data a real deployment must
      supply (e.g.\ ICU admission probability in the synthetic generator).
  \end{enumerate}
  \pause
  \footnotesize Every one of these lives on \texttt{PlanningInstance}
  (\texttt{src/model/types.py}), never hardcoded in solver logic -- a
  hospital overrides its own policy without touching the model.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Turnover and time budgets}
  \textbf{Room turnover} -- 15/25/40 min by duration bucket. Real OR
  turnover is reported anywhere from 15 to 60 minutes depending on
  procedure complexity and infection-control needs; bucketing by the
  case's own duration is a simple proxy for that spread (category 3/4 --
  a real deployment should use measured hospital data instead).
  \vspace{0.5em}

  \textbf{Surgeon limits} -- 240 min/day in the demo data is one standard
  half-day theatre session, a common unit in block-scheduling literature
  (Cardoen et al., 2010); 960 min/week leaves room for clinics, ward
  rounds, and on-call duties across the rest of a typical job plan
  (category 1/2: structurally grounded, not a single cited number).
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Max waits and priority multipliers}
  \textbf{Max waits} (270/60/15/3 days) -- Portugal's SIGIC policy,
  directly (category 1: literature-grounded -- this is the system
  presented two slides ago).
  \vspace{0.5em}

  \textbf{Priority multipliers} ($\mu_p = 1,\,4.5,\,18,\,90$) --
  \emph{derived}, not invented (category 2):
  \[
    \mu_p = \frac{\text{wl}^{max}_1}{\text{wl}^{max}_p}
    \;\Rightarrow\;
    \mu_2=\frac{270}{60}=4.5,\quad
    \mu_3=\frac{270}{15}=18,\quad
    \mu_4=\frac{270}{3}=90
  \]
  \pause
  A tier allowed only a twelfth of priority-1's wait (15 vs.\ 270 days) is,
  by the \emph{same} policy's own design, roughly twelve times more
  wait-sensitive -- weighting its breach days twelve times as heavily
  follows from the policy that set the wait targets, not a second,
  independent judgment call.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{The urgency multiplier $\alpha$ and the displacement margin}
  $\alpha=2.0$ appears only in Term 2's day coefficient -- it can only
  change \emph{which day} an overdue case lands on, never \emph{how many}
  get scheduled (capacity and Term 3 govern that, and neither involves
  $\alpha$).
  \vspace{0.5em}

  Term 3 ($w_c$) must dominate every Term-1/2 coefficient, or the model
  could prefer dropping a schedulable case to dodge a tardiness charge:
  \[
    \text{margin}_{\min} = 1 + \frac{\alpha\cdot n_{days}}{\max_c dd_c}
    \;\approx\; 1.037 \quad (\alpha=2,\ n_{days}=5,\ \max_c dd_c\approx270)
  \]
  \pause
  The model uses \textbf{1.2} -- clears the minimum with real margin, since
  $\max_c dd_c$ shrinks on instances with only short-horizon, high-priority
  cases, and a fixed margin has to stay safe across that range (category 2:
  derived, with an explicit safety buffer on top).
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Equipment and bed capacity}
  The demo's shared C-arm has capacity \textbf{1}, deliberately tight
  enough to force real contention among the four cases that need it --
  the point is to make the CP-vs-MIP gap (next section) actually visible,
  not to model one hospital's real inventory (category 3).
  \vspace{0.5em}

  ICU bed capacity (2/day demo, 6/day medium) follows the same logic:
  tight enough that long-stay cases create real pressure without making
  the instance infeasible. The overflow penalty $\pi^{ovf}=50$/day sits
  between two things it has to balance -- big enough to matter against a
  typical day-coefficient swing, small enough that it never beats simply
  not scheduling the case (which costs $w_c$, in the hundreds to low
  thousands).
  \vspace{0.5em}

  \footnotesize The 12\% ICU-admission rate in the synthetic generator is
  category 4 -- a placeholder for real admission data, never seen by the
  optimizer as a parameter at all.
\end{frame}

\section{Why CP-SAT, Not a Bigger MIP}

\begin{frame}{The core argument}
  \begin{block}{``Total minutes used today $\le$ capacity'' is not the same
    statement as ``these cases don't collide''}
    For one room, a set of non-overlapping durations can always be packed
    sequentially -- the two statements coincide. They stop coinciding the
    moment a resource is \emph{shared across rooms} (this project's C-arm).
  \end{block}
  \pause
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{NoOverlap} / \texttt{Cumulative} are global constraints
      with their own polynomial-time propagation (Vil\'im, 2004; Schutt et
      al., 2009) -- they prune from the problem's time structure directly.
    \item The MIP alternative (pairwise big-M disjunctions) is quadratic in
      conflicting pairs, and the big-M constants weaken the LP relaxation
      as that count grows (Baptiste, Le Pape \& Nuijten, 2001).
    \item CP-SAT's parallel portfolio search is the actual state of the art
      here -- no hand-rolled branching strategy on top of it.
  \end{itemize}
  \hyperlink{backupG}{\beamergotobutton{Full comparison-MIP formulation (backup)}}
\end{frame}

\section{Architecture \& Reusability}

\begin{frame}{How it's built}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{CP-SAT} (\texttt{cp\_sat\_interval\_solver.py}) -- the
      model, default and only backend needed to see it run.
    \item \textbf{Comparison MIP} (\texttt{milp\_baseline\_solver.py}) --
      CBC / SCIP / Gurobi / CPLEX, kept \emph{only} as the empirical check
      on the CP-over-MIP argument -- full math in the backup appendix, not
      presented as a second model.
    \item \textbf{CP Optimizer} (\texttt{cp\_optimizer\_solver.py},
      optional, license-gated) -- a second CP engine that can model
      sequence-dependent turnover; falls back to CP-SAT automatically.
    \item \texttt{tests/test\_model.py} -- the acceptance contract every
      solver's output is checked against (hard constraints, cross-solver
      agreement on the demo instance).
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{A reusable library of models}
  Four layers, solver-agnostic except the bottom one:
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Core data types} -- plain dataclasses, no solver imports.
    \item \textbf{Constraint patterns} -- capacity sums, NoOverlap-based
      non-overlap, tiered-priority tardiness, eligibility pre-filters --
      reusable across nurse rostering, bed allocation, this project.
    \item \textbf{Problem templates} -- compose those patterns into a
      specific formulation. This project is one template.
    \item \textbf{Solver-adapter layer} -- one file per backend family
      (MILP, CP, local search), so a new problem picks a backend without
      rewriting how constraints are expressed.
  \end{enumerate}
  \footnotesize The CP-vs-MIP comparison this project runs end to end is
  itself the template for layer 4: argue the backend choice from the
  problem's structure, then check it empirically, before committing.
\end{frame}

\section{Backup: The Comparison MIP (Appendix Model)}

\begin{frame}{Backup G -- the comparison MIP, in full}
  \hypertarget{backupG}{}
  Same sets and shared parameters as the CP-SAT model. Variables:
  \[
    x_{cdr}\in\{0,1\}\ \forall c,d\in D_c,r \qquad z_c\in[0,1]\ \forall c:p_c\ne4
  \]
  Objective -- identical shape to CP-SAT's Terms 1--3:
  \[
    \min \sum_{c:dd_c\ge0}\sum_{d,r}[dd_c+d]\,x_{cdr}
    + \sum_{c:dd_c<0}\sum_{d,r}[dd_c+\alpha d]\,x_{cdr}
    + \sum_{c:p_c\ne4} w_c\,z_c
  \]
  Same C1--C6, C9 as CP-SAT (room-service roster etc.\ pre-filtered the
  same way). Diverges at C7 and C10.
  \backbutton
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Backup G -- where the MIP diverges, and why there's no C11}
  \textbf{C7 -- room capacity as a sum, not exact non-overlap:}
  \[
    \sum_c t_c^{tot}\,x_{cdr}\le k_{dr} \qquad \forall d,r
  \]
  For a single room this is equivalent to exact non-overlap -- any
  non-colliding set of durations can always be packed sequentially.

  \textbf{C10 -- shared equipment, a day-level headcount, not exact
  overlap:}
  \[
    \sum_{c:u_{ce}=1}\sum_r x_{cdr}\le\kappa_{ed} \qquad \forall e,d
  \]
  This counts \emph{how many} equipment-$e$ cases land on a day, not
  whether their clock times overlap -- the single largest source of the
  objective gap measured earlier.

  \textbf{No C11.} A day-bucket model has no value that means ``day of
  surgery'' for a multi-day bed stay to start from. This, more than the
  equipment gap, is the structural reason this project ended up CP-based.
  \backbutton
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Backup H -- CP Optimizer (optional second CP engine)}
  Kept for one reason: sequence-dependent turnover, which CP-SAT can't
  express without restructuring its intervals.
  \begin{center}
  \footnotesize
  \begin{tabular}{lcc}
    \toprule
    & Same service, back-to-back & Different service, back-to-back \\
    \midrule
    CP-SAT (duration-bucketed) & charged on the case alone & same \\
    CP Optimizer (transition matrix) & 15 min & 35 min \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
  \end{center}
  \vspace{0.5em}
  Demo instance: identical objective to CP-SAT (155.0, 20/20). At the
  200-case scale its own gap closes far more slowly -- a search-tuning
  gap (no warm start applied), not a modeling one. Stays an appendix
  backend, not the model.
  \backbutton
\end{frame}
